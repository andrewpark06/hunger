Feeding a baby

What's actually safe to feed an infant

This section exists because the rules for feeding a baby are different from feeding anyone else in the house, and the consequences of guessing wrong can prove fatal.

0–6 months

Breast milk or formula is the only food a baby this age needs

According to the Mayo Clinic, newborns need breastmilk or formula, with 8 to 12 feedings a day. Look for signs for when the baby is hungry, and trust the process.

If money's tight

Never water down formula to make it last longer

According to the North Carolina Medical Society, diluting formula can cause water intoxication due to their underdeveloped kidneys. The golden rule is one scoop of formula and two ounces of water, properly mixed.

6–24 months

Solid foods add on, they don't replace milk or formula

Around 6 months, introduce small, bite-sized fruits, vegetables, and meats, and yogurt with no added sugar, along with whole grains. Avoid foods that have high added sugar, unpasteurized drinks or foods, and avoid cow's milk before 12 months.

Always

No honey before age 1, and reduce salt and sugars

Honey can carry a bacteria that's dangerous for infants. Added salt and sugar aren't needed at all in the first year: a baby's body is still learning to regulate both.

Watch for

Signs a baby isn't getting enough

  • Fewer than 6 wet diapers a day after the first week
  • Unusually sleepy, weak, or hard to wake for feeding
  • Not gaining weight between checkups, or losing weight
  • Constant fussiness that feeding doesn't settle

If you notice any of these, call your pediatrician or WIC nurse the same day.

If you've already substituted something other than breast milk or formula Please call your pediatrician, WIC office, or the FoodSource Hotline today and tell them exactly what happened. Acting fast matters.

For every age

Nutrition basics worth knowing

Beyond infants — practical, no-judgment basics for feeding a household when money, time, or both are tight.

Protein doesn't have to mean meat

Eggs, beans, lentils, peanut butter, and canned tuna are cheaper per serving than most meat and just as nutritionally solid.

Frozen and canned count as real produce

Frozen vegetables are often picked and frozen at peak ripeness — they can be more nutritious than out-of-season fresh produce, and they keep far longer.

Reading a label fast

Check serving size first — it changes everything else on the label. Then scan sodium and added sugar; both add up quickly in processed food.

SNAP stretches further than people expect

SNAP benefits can be used at farmers markets, and many MA markets double the value through the Healthy Incentives Program (HIP).

A pantry visit isn't a last resort

Food pantries are built for regular, ongoing use — not just emergencies. Using one doesn't mean you've failed at anything.

Toddlers need fat, not less of it

Unlike adult diets, toddlers' growing brains need a higher proportion of fat — whole milk, avocado, and olive oil are appropriate, not excessive.

Help keep this directory current

This started as one person's response to a death that didn't have to happen. It only stays useful if the people who actually run pantries, clinics, and WIC offices help keep it accurate — and if neighbors help it reach the people who need it.

  • Tell us if a listing is wrong. Hours and phone numbers change — a five-minute correction can save someone a wasted trip.
  • Print the resource list. Take a printed copy to a laundromat, clinic waiting room, or church bulletin board.
  • If you work in healthcare or social work, tell us what families ask you for most — it shapes what we build next.
  • If you've been through this yourself, and are willing to share what would have helped, that input matters more than ours.
